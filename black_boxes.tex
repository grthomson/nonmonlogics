\section{Introduction}
Sutton's \emph{Bitter Lesson} is the observation that most significant advances in artificial intelligence have been made by leveraging increases in processing power rather than by the development of novel algorithms to implement specialised or domain-specific knowledge. Wang-Ma\'{s}cianica (2023) points out that in the case of Natural Language Processing, it is formal semantics -- understood here as a subfield of linguistics -- that has been most embarrassed by the Bitter Lesson. The traditional logic-based and compositional approach to the analysis of natural language is conspicuously absent in the story of Large Language Models:

\begin{quote} The tragedy goes like this:
there's a group of people who investigate language - from syntax and semantics to pragmatics and analogies and storytelling and slang - who treat their subject with formal rigour and have been at it for many centuries. Their role in the story of LLMs is remarkable because it doesn't exist. 
\begin{flushright} Wang-Ma\'{s}cianica (2023, p.13)\end{flushright}
\end{quote}

\noindent In this essay I discuss the relationship between LLMs understood as tools for generating meaningful linguistic items, and formal semantics understood as a mathematical-logical model of language. I describe current \emph{distributional semantics} for LLMs and explain why that model is in tension with formal or logical semantics. I further argue that LLMs are not incommensurable with formal semantics, but that the mathematical framework must be enriched if we are to reconcile our best theories of linguistic meaning with the probabilistic or frequency-based methods of LLM text generation. 

The structure of the essay is as follows. I first describe some common desiderata for a formal semantics as understood by linguists, and I explain how these desiderata are met by both reference-based and inference-based approaches. Here I discuss briefly the relationship between formal semantics in linguistics, and semantics for logics and for programming languages. I then describe a BERT-style semantics for language models and explain why this fails to meet the common desiderata. In this section I introduce also the philosophical idea of a ``theory of meaning" and I explain how, unlike traditional formal semantics, distributional semantics for LLMs are not straightforwardly amenable to any human-friendly theory of meaning. In the final sections I discuss the status of ``nonmonotonic" reasoning -- reasoning which is not expressible in a deductive logic admitting a structural \emph{Weakening} rule -- in current formal semantics. I sketch a novel mathematical framework for nonmonotonic natural language semantics which is compatible with LLM operations. Here I draw on the tools of Hopf algebra and (higher) category theory. I reference recent work by Chomsky and Marcolli (2023) which applies the Loday-Ronco Hopf algebra to the syntactic merge operation, and earlier work by D\v{o}sen (1999) on categories and cut elimination in logic. On the Hopf algebra side, the Loday-Ronco algebra is used to model the splitting and merging of proof trees for a nonmonotonic sequent calculus. The coalgebraic structure of the Hopf algebra models the relation of \emph{decomposition} between the canonical derivation of a sequent and the set of all (sub-)derivations, extending the ordinary \emph{compositional} model of a logic in terms of (categories of) partial orders. I argue that a compositional-decompositional model of a logic (or consequence relation) is necessary to define an equivalence between the directed structure of a proof tree and the undirected structure of an LLM embedding. On the category-theoretic side, I define the ``syntactic" category $\mathbf{Tree_{NonMon}}$ of nonmonotonic proof trees and the ``semantic" category $\mathbf{V_{Embed}}$ of LLM vector space embeddings. A category-theoretic definition of the structural rule \emph{Cautious Cut} is given and proven admissible for $\mathbf{Tree_{NonMon}}$. A guiding idea here is that there should be a ``lossy'' mapping between natural language inference and LLM inference - this is modelled by i) composition of the Hopf algebra antipode with an embedding morphism, and ii) an adjunction between $\mathbf{Tree_{NonMon}}$ and $\mathbf{V_{Embed}}$. The mathematical framework sketched is naturally understood as compatible with a proof-theoretic or inferential treatment of linguistic meaning.
