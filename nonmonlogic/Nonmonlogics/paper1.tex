\begin{abstract}
We define algebraic structure on proof trees arising from a nonmonotonic
multi-succedent sequent calculus. The primitive combinatorial operation is
a matching-leaf grafting operation, which composes derivations by replacing
a leaf of one proof tree with another proof tree having the same concluding
sequent. This yields a natural candidate pre-Lie product on proof trees.
A second, cut-based operation yields a coproduct encoding decomposition into
subderivations. Since raw address-level witness counting contains
bureaucratic artefacts, we pass to a quotient identifying proof presentations
that differ only by inessential scheduling data. At the quotient level we
obtain the pre-Lie identity and hence, via a standard enveloping construction,
a Hopf algebra of proof trees for a nonmonotonic sequent calculus.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The present paper develops an algebraic treatment of inference in a
nonmonotonic setting. Our guiding idea is that proof trees should carry both
a \emph{compositional} and a \emph{decompositional} structure. Composition
corresponds to grafting one derivation into another, while decomposition
corresponds to extracting subderivations by admissible cuts. The resulting
interaction is naturally Hopf-algebraic.

The proof-theoretic background is a nonmonotonic sequent calculus. The
failure of weakening is essential: if inference is not stable under
arbitrary enlargement of the premise set, then proof composition cannot be
treated as a free combinatorial operation. Instead, composition must respect
logical interfaces, here formalized by matching conclusion sequents.

This gives a proof-theoretically constrained analogue of the familiar
combinatorial Hopf algebras on rooted trees. The trees considered here are
not abstract rooted trees, but proof trees generated from a sequent calculus
with a primitive base consequence relation. Their logical decoration is
therefore constitutive of the algebraic structure rather than external to it.

Our eventual aim is to show that, after quotienting away proof-theoretically
inessential bureaucracy, the grafting operation satisfies the pre-Lie
identity. This yields a Hopf algebra of derivations in the sense relevant to
nonmonotonic inference. In this way we treat logical constants and derivation
patterns not merely as syntactic devices but as algebraically organised
features of inference itself.

\section{The Nonmonotonic Sequent Calculus}

\subsection{Formulas and sequents}

We begin with a simple propositional language.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathsf{At}$ be a set of atomic symbols. The set $\mathcal{L}$ of
formulas is generated by the grammar
\[
A ::= p \mid \bot \mid (A \Rightarrow A) \mid (A \wedge A) \mid (A \vee A)
\mid \neg A,
\]
where $p \in \mathsf{At}$.
\end{definition}

The constant $\bot$ plays a special role as an incompatibility marker. At
this stage we do not impose any global principle identifying derivability of
$\bot$ with inconsistency via a deduction theorem. Rather, incompatibility is
tracked proof-theoretically through the primitive consequence relation.

\begin{definition}
A \emph{multi-succedent sequent} is an expression
\[
\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Delta,
\]
where $\Gamma$ and $\Delta$ are finite multisets of formulas.
\end{definition}

The use of multisets reflects that structural rules are not built in by
default. In particular, we do not assume weakening.

\subsection{Primitive consequence relation}

\begin{definition}
A \emph{base consequence relation} is a relation
\[
\mathsf{base} \subseteq \mathsf{MSet}(\mathcal{L}) \times \mathsf{MSet}(\mathcal{L}).
\]
Equivalently, for multisets $\Gamma,\Theta$ of formulas, one may write
$\mathsf{base}(\Gamma,\Theta)$.
\end{definition}

The base relation supplies the leaf-level proof-theoretic data. It should be
thought of as the primitive inferential background over which the sequent
rules generate derivations.

\subsection{Derivations}

Fix a base consequence relation $\mathsf{base}$. We define a derivability
relation inductively.

\begin{definition}
The derivations of the nonmonotonic multi-succedent sequent calculus
$\mathsf{NMMS}(\mathsf{base})$ are generated by the following clauses.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Base axiom.}
For any multisets $\Gamma,\Theta$,
\[
\frac{\mathsf{base}(\Gamma,\Theta)}{\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Theta}
\quad \text{(Base)}
\]

\paragraph{Implication rules.}
\[
\frac{\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} A,\Delta
\qquad
B,\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Delta}
{(A \Rightarrow B),\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Delta}
\quad (\Rightarrow L)
\]
\[
\frac{A,\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} B,\Delta}
{\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} (A \Rightarrow B),\Delta}
\quad (\Rightarrow R)
\]

\paragraph{Conjunction rules.}
\[
\frac{A,B,\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Delta}
{(A \wedge B),\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Delta}
\quad (\wedge L)
\]
\[
\frac{\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} A,\Delta
\qquad
\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} B,\Delta}
{\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} (A \wedge B),\Delta}
\quad (\wedge R)
\]

\paragraph{Disjunction rules.}
\[
\frac{A,\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Delta
\qquad
B,\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Delta}
{(A \vee B),\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Delta}
\quad (\vee L)
\]
\[
\frac{\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} A,B,\Delta}
{\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} (A \vee B),\Delta}
\quad (\vee R)
\]

\paragraph{Negation rules.}
\[
\frac{\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} A,\Delta}
{\neg A,\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Delta}
\quad (\neg L)
\qquad
\frac{A,\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Delta}
{\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \neg A,\Delta}
\quad (\neg R)
\]

\begin{remark}
No weakening rule is assumed. In particular, the rules
\[
\frac{\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Delta}
{\Gamma,A \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Delta}
\quad
\frac{\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Delta}
{\Gamma \mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Delta,A}
\]
are not taken to be admissible in general. This is the formal expression of
the nonmonotonic character of the system.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}
Likewise, we do not assume unrestricted cut-elimination as part of the
background. The algebraic structures developed below are intended precisely
for a setting in which composition and decomposition of derivations must be
handled in a controlled, proof-theoretically sensitive way.
\end{remark}

\section{Proof Trees}

\subsection{Plain proof trees}

\begin{definition}
A \emph{plain proof tree} is a rooted tree whose nodes are labelled by
sequents and whose internal nodes are further tagged by one of the rule
symbols
\[
\mathsf{Base},\ \Rightarrow L,\ \Rightarrow R,\ \wedge L,\ \wedge R,\
\vee L,\ \vee R,\ \neg L,\ \neg R.
\]
Leaves carry only a concluding sequent.
\end{definition}

Thus a proof tree forgets the specific derivation object while retaining the
inferential shape of the derivation, including the sequent labelling at each
node and the rule applied at each interior node.

\begin{definition}
If $t$ is a proof tree, we write:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt]
\item $\mathrm{conc}(t)$ for its concluding sequent,
\item $\mathrm{size}(t)$ for its number of nodes,
\item $\mathrm{ht}(t)$ for its height,
\item $\mathrm{children}(t)$ for its immediate subtrees.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
An \emph{address} in a proof tree is a finite list of natural numbers,
indicating a path from the root to a subtree.
\end{definition}

Addresses allow us to refer to a specific subtree or leaf of a proof tree.
They play a technical role in defining local grafting operations, though the
final algebraic constructions will quotient away inessential address-level
bureaucracy.

\subsection{From derivations to proof trees}

Every derivation in $\mathsf{NMMS}(\mathsf{base})$ determines a proof tree by
forgetting the derivation witness and retaining only the rule-labelled tree
of sequents.

\begin{definition}
Let $d$ be a derivation of a sequent $s$. The associated proof tree
\[
\mathrm{toTree}(d)
\]
is defined recursively by replacing each inference step by the corresponding
rule-labelled node and each base derivation by a leaf.
\end{definition}

Concretely, if $d$ ends with an implication-left step, then
$\mathrm{toTree}(d)$ has root label $(A \Rightarrow B),\Gamma
\mathrel{\mid\!\sim} \Theta$ and two children given by the proof trees of the
premiss derivations; similarly for the other rules.

\begin{lemma}
For every derivation $d$ of a sequent $s$, the conclusion of its associated
proof tree is $s$:
\[
\mathrm{conc}(\mathrm{toTree}(d)) = s.
\]
\end{lemma}

\section{Matching-Leaf Grafting}

\subsection{Local grafting}

The basic compositional operation in our framework is the replacement of a
leaf of one proof tree by another proof tree with the same concluding
sequent.

\begin{definition}
Let $u$ and $t$ be proof trees, and let $a$ be an address in $t$. We say that
$a$ is a \emph{graftable leaf address for $u$ in $t$} if the subtree of $t$ at
address $a$ is a leaf labelled by the sequent $\mathrm{conc}(u)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
Suppose $a$ is a graftable leaf address for $u$ in $t$. The
\emph{matching-leaf grafting of $u$ into $t$ at $a$}, denoted
\[
\mathrm{graft}_a(u,t),
\]
is the proof tree obtained from $t$ by replacing the leaf at address $a$ by
the tree $u$.
\end{definition}

Equivalently, grafting is defined only when the conclusion of the inserted
tree matches the sequent labelling the target leaf.

\begin{definition}
The set of all matching-leaf graftings of $u$ into $t$ is
\[
\mathrm{Graft}(u,t)
=
\{\, \mathrm{graft}_a(u,t) \mid a \text{ is a graftable leaf address for }
u \text{ in } t \,\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\subsection{Closure on derivation trees}

A crucial proof-theoretic fact is that grafting derivation trees does not
leave the world of derivation trees.

\begin{theorem}[Closure of matching-leaf grafting under derivability]
Let $d_{\mathrm{out}}$ and $d_{\mathrm{in}}$ be derivations in
$\mathsf{NMMS}(\mathsf{base})$, with proof trees
$\mathrm{toTree}(d_{\mathrm{out}})$ and $\mathrm{toTree}(d_{\mathrm{in}})$.
If $a$ is a graftable leaf address for $\mathrm{toTree}(d_{\mathrm{in}})$ in
$\mathrm{toTree}(d_{\mathrm{out}})$, then there exists a derivation $d'$ such
that
\[
\mathrm{graft}_a\bigl(\mathrm{toTree}(d_{\mathrm{in}}),
\mathrm{toTree}(d_{\mathrm{out}})\bigr)
=
\mathrm{toTree}(d').
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}
This theorem justifies reading matching-leaf grafting as genuine proof
composition rather than a merely combinatorial tree operation. The operation
respects the inductive generation of derivations by the sequent rules.
\end{remark}

\section{Two-Step Grafting and the Geometry of the Associator}

We now analyse iterated matching-leaf grafting. This is the structural core of
the pre-Lie analysis, since the pre-Lie identity is controlled by the
difference between the two iterated products
\[
x \triangleright (y \triangleright z)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
(x \triangleright y) \triangleright z.
\]

At the raw tree level, the relevant combinatorics is governed by the
interaction of two graft addresses inside a common ambient tree.

\subsection{Dependent and independent two-step graftings}

Suppose first that $y$ is grafted into $z$ at an address $a$, producing a tree
$z'$, and then $x$ is grafted into $z'$ at an address $b$, producing a tree
$w$. There are only two possibilities: either the second graft lands inside the
subtree previously inserted at $a$, or it takes place at an address independent
of $a$.

\begin{theorem}[Full two-step graft decomposition]
Let $x,y,z$ be proof trees. Suppose that grafting $y$ into $z$ at an address
$a$ yields a tree $z'$, and grafting $x$ into $z'$ at an address $b$ yields a
tree $w$. Then one of the following two geometric situations occurs.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\roman*)}]
\item \textbf{Nested case.} There exist an address $c$ and a proof tree $y'$
such that
\[
b = a \mathbin{\raisebox{0.2ex}{\scalebox{0.8}{$\frown$}}} c,
\]
and such that grafting $x$ into $y$ at $c$ yields $y'$, while grafting $y'$
into $z$ at $a$ yields $w$.

\item \textbf{Independent case.} There exists a proof tree $z_3$ such that the
addresses $a$ and $b$ are incomparable, grafting $x$ into $z$ at $b$ yields
$z_3$, and grafting $y$ into $z_3$ at $a$ yields $w$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}
The theorem says that every successful two-step grafting factors either through
a genuine insertion of $x$ into the already-inserted tree $y$, or through two
independent insertions into disjoint regions of $z$. This dichotomy is the
proof-tree analogue of the familiar decomposition of rooted-tree insertions
into nested and disjoint contributions.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Commutation of independent graftings}

The independent case admits a stronger formulation: incomparable successful
grafts commute.

\begin{theorem}[Commutation of incomparable graftings]
Let $x,y,z$ be proof trees, and let $a,b$ be incomparable addresses in $z$.
Suppose that grafting $y$ into $z$ at $a$ produces $z_1$, and grafting $x$
into $z_1$ at $b$ produces $z_2$. Suppose also that grafting $x$ into $z$ at
$b$ produces $z_3$. Then grafting $y$ into $z_3$ at $a$ is defined and yields
the same final tree:
\[
\mathrm{graft}_a(y,z_3)=z_2.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}
This is the precise sense in which independent proof insertions commute. The
result does not say that grafting is globally commutative; rather, it says that
noninteracting local insertions can be permuted without changing the resulting
proof tree.
\end{remark}

\section{The Free Abelian Group on Proof Trees and the Raw Grafting Product}

We now pass from individual proof trees to formal linear combinations of proof
trees.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathbb{Z}\langle \mathsf{PTree}\rangle$ denote the free abelian group on
the set of proof trees. For a proof tree $t$, write $[t]$ for the corresponding
basis element.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Tree-level grafting product]
For proof trees $u$ and $t$, define
\[
u \triangleright t
:=
\sum_{v \in \mathrm{Graft}(u,t)} [v]
\;\in\;
\mathbb{Z}\langle \mathsf{PTree}\rangle.
\]
Equivalently, $u \triangleright t$ is the formal sum of all proof trees
obtained by grafting $u$ into $t$ at a matching leaf.
\end{definition}

This is the primitive product from which the pre-Lie structure will be
obtained after quotienting the appropriate witness data.

\begin{proposition}[Compatibility with generators]
The bilinear grafting product is determined on basis elements by the tree-level
grafting operation. In particular, for proof trees $u$ and $t$,
\[
[u] \triangleright [t]
=
u \triangleright t.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}
This is formally straightforward, but mathematically important: the product on
the free abelian group is not an additional structure but the linear extension
of matching-leaf grafting on individual proof trees.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Iterated grafting and coefficient formulas}

The next step is to express iterated grafting in terms of counts of two-step
graftings.

\begin{proposition}[Expansion of left action over a graft sum]
For proof trees $u,t,w$,
\[
u \triangleright (t \triangleright w)
=
\sum_{z' \in \mathrm{Graft}(t,w)} u \triangleright z'.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Coefficient formula for $x \triangleright (y \triangleright z)$]
Let $x,y,z,w$ be proof trees. Then the coefficient of $[w]$ in
$x \triangleright (y \triangleright z)$ is the number of two-step graftings of
the following form: first graft $y$ into $z$, obtaining some intermediate tree
$z'$, and then graft $x$ into $z'$, obtaining $w$. Explicitly,
\[
[w]\bigl(x \triangleright (y \triangleright z)\bigr)
=
\#\Bigl\{
z' \in \mathrm{Graft}(y,z)
\;\Big|\;
w \in \mathrm{Graft}(x,z')
\Bigr\}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Coefficient formula for $(x \triangleright y)\triangleright z$]
Let $x,y,z,w$ be proof trees. Then the coefficient of $[w]$ in
$(x \triangleright y)\triangleright z$ is the number of two-step graftings of
the following form: first graft $x$ into $y$, obtaining some intermediate tree
$y'$, and then graft $y'$ into $z$, obtaining $w$. Explicitly,
\[
[w]\bigl((x \triangleright y)\triangleright z\bigr)
=
\#\Bigl\{
y' \in \mathrm{Graft}(x,y)
\;\Big|\;
w \in \mathrm{Graft}(y',z)
\Bigr\}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}
These coefficient formulas show that the pre-Lie identity is fundamentally a
comparison between two families of two-step grafting witnesses. The remainder
of the argument therefore focuses on organising, comparing, and quotienting
those witnesses.
\end{remark}

\section{The Coproduct Side}

Alongside the grafting product, the theory also carries a decomposition
operation coming from admissible cuts of proof trees.

\begin{definition}
For each proof tree $t$, let $\Delta(t)$ denote the collection of all
decompositions of $t$ arising from admissible cuts. Each such cut yields a pair
consisting of a forest of extracted subtrees and a remainder tree.
\end{definition}

The precise admissibility conditions on cuts were introduced elsewhere in the
formal development; for the present discussion it is enough to note that the
empty cut is always admissible.

\begin{proposition}[Nonemptiness of the coproduct data]
For every proof tree $t$, the coproduct data associated with $t$ is nonempty.
Equivalently, $\Delta(t)$ always contains at least one term.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Idea of proof]
The empty cut is admissible. Its corresponding decomposition contributes the
trivial term consisting of the empty forest together with the original tree
$t$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
This is a minimal but conceptually useful fact: the decomposition structure is
never vacuous. In particular, the cut-based coalgebra has a canonical trivial
term, just as in the usual rooted-tree coproducts.
\end{remark}

\section{Two-Step Codes and Bureaucratic Equivalence}

The coefficient formulas above reduce the pre-Lie problem to a comparison of
two-step grafting data. At raw address level, however, this data contains
bureaucratic artefacts: the same underlying proof-theoretic interaction may be
presented by different address choices or by different orders of independent
insertions.

To remove this noise we introduce an equivalence relation on two-step witness
codes.

\subsection{Two-step codes}

\begin{definition}
Fix proof trees $x,y,z,w$. A \emph{two-step code} for the production of $w$
from $x,y,z$ records one of the possible geometric shapes of a successful
two-step grafting. In particular, one distinguishes:
\begin{itemize}
\item left-outer codes,
\item right-outer codes,
\item left-inner codes,
\item right-inner codes.
\end{itemize}
These encode whether one starts from the left- or right-associated iterated
product, and whether the second insertion is independent of or nested inside
the first.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
The exact coding carries address-level witness data and intermediate trees.
That detail is important for the formal proof, but for the mathematical
presentation the essential point is that two-step codes classify successful
two-step graftings by their geometric type.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Equivalence generated by commuting outer contributions}

\begin{definition}[Two-step equivalence]
Fix proof trees $x,y,z,w$. The relation
\[
\sim_{x,y,z,w}
\]
on two-step codes is the least equivalence relation generated by the following
identifications.

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item A left-outer code is identified with any right-outer code representing
the same proof-theoretic contribution obtained by commuting independent outer
insertions.

\item A left-outer code is identified with any right-inner code representing
the same final contribution after transport across the corresponding two-step
geometry.

\item Symmetrically, a right-outer code is identified with any left-outer code
obtained by commuting independent outer insertions.

\item Symmetrically, a right-outer code is identified with any left-inner code
representing the same contribution after the corresponding transport.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}
This relation is designed to identify exactly those two-step presentations that
differ only by inessential bureaucratic data. The generators correspond to the
basic proof-theoretic symmetries discovered in the analysis of two-step
grafting.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}
The \emph{two-step quotient} for $x,y,z,w$ is the set of equivalence classes of
two-step codes under $\sim_{x,y,z,w}$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Existence of opposite-side representatives}

One of the first structural consequences of the quotient construction is that
an outer witness on one side always determines some representative on the
opposite side.

\begin{lemma}[Left-outer classes admit right-side representatives]
Let $x,y,z,w$ be proof trees, and let a successful left-outer two-step grafting
from $(x,y,z)$ to $w$ be given. Then there exists a two-step code on the right
side such that the original left-outer code is equivalent to it in the two-step
quotient.
\end{lemma}

\begin{remark}
This lemma is the beginning of the comparison theorem between the two
parenthesisations. It does not yet assert uniqueness or purity, but it shows
that left-associated outer contributions are never isolated: each is linked by
the quotient to some right-associated contribution.
\end{remark}

\section{Towards the Class-Level Pre-Lie Identity}

The quotient construction is introduced because raw witness-counting does not
yet isolate the genuinely proof-theoretic content of the associator.
Independent insertions may be represented by several bureaucratically distinct
address patterns, and these must be identified before a clean algebraic
identity emerges.

At the quotient level, the intended picture is that every contribution class in
the associator decomposes into three sectors:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textup{(\arabic*)}]
\item quotient-trivial noise,
\item pure outer residual classes,
\item pure inner residual classes.
\end{enumerate}

The decisive further step, to be developed in the next stage of the paper, is
to prove that mixed residual classes disappear, so that the remaining quotient
classes are pure and typed. This allows one to compare the two
parenthesisations class by class and derive the quotient-level pre-Lie
identity.

\section{Hopf-Algebraic Outlook}

Once the quotient-level pre-Lie identity has been established, one may pass
from the primitive pre-Lie structure to an enveloping Hopf algebra by a
standard construction of Oudom--Guin type. In parallel, the admissible-cut
decomposition of proof trees supplies a coproduct reflecting the internal
subderivation structure of proofs.

The resulting object should therefore be understood as a Hopf algebra of
nonmonotonic proof trees: the algebra structure models composition of proofs,
while the coalgebra structure models decomposition into subproofs. This is the
sense in which the present framework seeks to capture both the constructive and
the analytic aspects of inference within a single algebraic object.

\section{Conclusion}

We have assembled the proof-theoretic and combinatorial foundations for a Hopf
algebraic treatment of derivations in a nonmonotonic sequent calculus. The
basic objects are proof trees generated from a primitive base consequence
relation. Their local composition is given by matching-leaf grafting, and the
analysis of iterated grafting leads naturally to a quotient on two-step witness
data identifying bureaucratically equivalent proof presentations.

What remains is to complete the structural analysis of quotient classes, prove
the class-level pre-Lie identity, and integrate this with the cut-based
coproduct in a full Hopf algebra construction. Even at the present stage,
however, the framework already displays the central idea: nonmonotonic
inference admits a rich algebraic organisation in which proof composition and
proof decomposition are treated on an equal footing.
